SECTION 3: PROPOSAL STRUCTURE GUIDE

How to Write a Proposal That Closes

✓ SECTION 1 — OPEN WITH THE VISION (not the price):

✓ Write 2-3 evocative sentences that paint the picture of the dining experience. Use

sensory language. Make the client see, smell, and taste it before they read a single detail.

✓ Example: 'Imagine your guests arriving to the scent of slow-roasted lamb and wild

herbs, a table set with candlelight and fresh flowers, and an evening that unfolds as naturally as conversation between old friends.'

✓

✓ SECTION 2 — THE MENU:

✓ List each course with a brief, evocative description. Use proper culinary language

but make it accessible. Mention key ingredients and the flavour profile, not just the technique.

✓ Note dietary accommodations clearly — show that you've genuinely listened.

✓

✓ Describe exactly what is included: grocery sourcing, preparation timeline, service

style (plated / family-style / buffet), course timing, cleanup. Remove all ambiguity.

✓

✓ SECTION 4 — THE INVESTMENT:

✓ Present the investment confidently and clearly. Break it into components if helpful —

chef fee, grocery budget, travel. Never apologise for the price. State what it includes.

✓

✓ SECTION 5 — THE NEXT STEP:

✓ A proposal without a clear next step rarely closes. State exactly what happens next:

'To confirm your booking, I require a [X]% deposit of $[amount] via [method]. I'll hold [date] for [X days] while you review.'

✓

✓ [The chef who writes a proposal that makes the client feel the experience before

they see the price is the chef who closes. Vision first, investment second — always.]
