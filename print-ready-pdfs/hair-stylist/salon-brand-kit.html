What Is a Brand Kit?

A brand kit is a reference document that captures all the decisions about how your business

presents itself. When you have one, every piece of content you create — a caption, a price increase

email, a job posting, a TikTok script — sounds and looks like it came from the same place. Without

one, you are reinventing yourself every time you post.

Section 1

Brand Foundation

Your brand foundation is the strategic layer that everything else is built on. Get this right and your

voice, visuals, and content will feel coherent naturally.

Step 1 — Your Positioning Statement

A positioning statement is one or two sentences that capture who you serve, what you do, and

what makes you different. It is not your tagline — it is the internal compass you use when making

brand decisions.

The Formula:

I help [target client] achieve [desired outcome] through [your approach/differentiator] — without

[common pain point they want to avoid].

Example (fill this in for your own salon):

I help busy women in their 30s and 40s get low-maintenance color that grows out beautifully —

through techniques that prioritize the health of their hair, without the commitment of 6-week

touch-ups.

Section 2

Brand Voice

Your brand voice is how your business sounds when it speaks. It is consistent across every caption,

text, email, and response to a review. Clients who love your voice will come looking for it — and it

is one of the hardest things for competitors to copy.

Step 1 — Choose Your 3 Voice Attributes

Pick 3 adjectives from the list below that describe how you want your brand to sound. For each

one, add what it means in practice and what it does NOT mean:

Warm · Direct · Expert · Playful · Confident · Nurturing · Edgy · Premium · Approachable · Educational ·

NEVER

Use contractions — "you're", "I'm", "we've"

Start captions with "I" (Instagram algorithm penalizes this)

Write like you talk behind the chair

Use corporate words: "utilize", "leverage", "solutions"

Use the second person: "you", "your hair"

Write in passive voice: "appointments are available" vs. "I have openings"

Photography & Imagery Direction

Your photo aesthetic should be consistent. Answer these questions and you will have a

photography brief you can use forever:

Lighting: Natural light or warm artificial? Bright and airy or moody and dramatic?

Background: Clean white/cream, salon setting, outdoor, or dark backdrop?

Color grading: Warm tones, cool tones, desaturated film look, or true-to-life color?

Composition: Close-up detail shots, full head, client facing forward or turned?

Props & Setting: Minimalist (just the hair), styled flat lays, tools in frame?

Client styling: Styled and polished, or natural/undone — which fits your brand?
